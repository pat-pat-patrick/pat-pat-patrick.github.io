*How rising yields could force the White House to compromise—and when investors should watch for it.*

- **Current context:** The Federal Reserve is at a critical juncture, deciding whether to cut rates more. **Any new inflationary shock is intolerable**, as it would prevent easing and could force tightening instead.
- **Treasury yields spike → financing costs soar.** Even a modest increase in interest rates can trigger **explosive growth in debt service**, threatening fiscal stability.
- **Interest expense is the real red line.** Surges in debt service cannot be tolerated; it is the ultimate constraint on policy.

---

When Donald Trump threatened to impose an additional 100% tariff on Chinese imports, markets immediately sensed danger. The timing of this threat is especially delicate, coming just as the Federal Reserve debates whether to cut interest rates.
Even if such tariffs trigger only a one-time spike in inflation, they still add to the Fed’s dilemma: it cannot ease monetary policy while fresh inflationary pressure looms. In short, **Trump’s own aggressiveness makes rate cuts nearly impossible.**

This is not the first time the market has reacted sharply to tariff threats. In April 2025, when the Trump administration expanded tariff rhetoric globally, the U.S. bond market plunged. Traders instantly recognized the implications: large-scale tariffs would fuel another round of uncontrolled inflation. To counter this, the Fed might be forced to raise rates again, sending Treasury yields soaring and bond prices crashing.
The episode revealed a fundamental truth: the real lifeline of the U.S. government is not the stock market—it’s the bond market. While stock declines mainly hurt corporate valuations and investor wealth, a bond market selloff strikes directly at the government’s fiscal heart.

## The Fiscal Weak Spot

As of August 2025, U.S. national debt stands at $37 trillion. For the 2025 fiscal year, interest payments alone are expected to reach $1.124 trillion, about **17%** of total federal spending. That makes interest costs the second-largest federal expense, behind only Social Security—surpassing both defense and Medicare.

This structural burden explains why a surge in the 10-year Treasury yield terrifies Washington. Every uptick in yields means higher refinancing costs for trillions of dollars in debt. If yields spiral out of control, the entire U.S. fiscal system could face a crisis. When that happens, ideology dissolves, and the government must compromise.

After all, a few billion lost in the stock market doesn’t matter. Investors can take the hit, and politicians can even profit from the chaos through well-timed trades. A few percentage points of inflation? Also tolerable, it can be sold as the “necessary sacrifice” for national renewal.
But a surge in interest expenses is intolerable. When the Treasury’s own financing costs explode, the guillotine swings toward Washington itself. Everything is negotiable until that blade falls.

And unlike April, when swollen inventories still cushioned price increases, today’s U.S. inventory levels have already bottomed. There’s little buffer left to absorb new tariff-driven shocks. This time, the inflation will hit faster, and bite harder.

## The Logic Behind Trump’s Economic Policy

The core logic of the Trump administration’s economic policy can be summarized in one sentence:
 economic growth, price stability, and even the defense of the dollar’s global dominance are negotiable—while manufacturing reshoring, fiscal consolidation, and constraining strategic rivals are nonnegotiable priorities.

Among these, **improving fiscal health and controlling spending** stand at the top of the hierarchy. Behind the administration’s seemingly erratic or confrontational moves lies a consistent objective: cutting costs. Whether by pressuring the Fed to lower rates, imposing tariffs to boost domestic revenue, or reshaping global supply chains, every move ultimately points toward reducing the government’s financial burden. This fiscal discipline, sometimes masked as protectionism or nationalism, is the underlying logic behind Trump’s unpredictability.

### **Why Trump Wants to Control the Fed**

Trump’s push to influence or “control” the Federal Reserve is therefore less about personal ego and more about **fiscal survival**. By gaining leverage over the Fed, Trump could **force lower interest rates**, directly cutting borrowing costs and easing the government’s most painful expenditure: debt service. Controlling the cost of money effectively means controlling the pace of fiscal bleeding.

### **The Anthony Nomination as a Political Signal**

The failed nomination of Anthony, an economist from the conservative Heritage Foundation, to lead the Bureau of Labor Statistics, provides an important political signal. The Heritage Foundation, one of the most influential think tanks shaping Trump-era policy, advocates deregulation, reduced government oversight, and an assertive stance toward China.

Anthony’s rejection by both Democrats and Republicans shows where Congress draws the line. Any policy more radical than his would likely face bipartisan resistance. This episode defines the outer edge of what Washington will tolerate, and thus serves as a key indicator for evaluating future Trump policies.

### **The Market Indicator to Watch**

In the months ahead, the **10-year Treasury yield** remains the most critical gauge. If yields surge, it signals the market’s fear that inflation, or fiscal recklessness, is spiraling beyond control. That, in turn, marks the moment when the government will be forced to retreat or compromise.

For investors, that inflection point could also mark the bottoming of the equity market—the moment when political pressure meets market reality, and policy must bend.
